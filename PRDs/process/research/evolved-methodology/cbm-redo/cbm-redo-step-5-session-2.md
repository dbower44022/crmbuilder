## 3. Part B — Backbone Walkthrough

### 3.1 Summary

This was the heart of Session 2 and the longest part — about 65 minutes. The consultant presented the proposed backbone confidently, the client engaged substantively, and one material area of pushback emerged: the client objected to all of Donors/Funding being fully deferred. After focused discussion using the deferred-not-dismissed framing, the donor recording process was upgraded from "deferred" to "supporting" but not added to the backbone for iteration 1. The four mission-critical processes survived intact. The G-007 and G-008 process combinations were accepted with minor adjustments to wording.

The client's only other pushback was a specific clarifying concern about Engagement Closing: the client wanted to confirm that "supporting" did not mean "ignored," because closing-with-outcome-tracking matters for the impact reports CBM owes its donors. The consultant confirmed that supporting means "next iteration's candidate," not "indefinitely deferred."

### 3.2 Verbatim — confident proposal opening

> **Consultant:** I want to walk you through what we think is the mission-critical backbone for CBM — the smallest set of connected processes that lets a real person at your organization do the work end-to-end. Everything outside this set we'd defer to later iterations. Iteration 1 is about getting something running that you can use, react to, and iterate from.
>
> Here's what we've classified as mission-critical, four processes spanning three of your six domains:
>
> First, **Mentor Application & Initial Activation** in The Mentor Pool — taking in volunteer mentor applications, conducting screening, and producing mentor records marked available for matching.
>
> Second, **Client Application & Intake** in Bringing in Clients — taking in client applications, conducting the intake conversation, and producing client records marked ready for matching.
>
> Third, **Matching** in The Mentoring — pairing available mentors with ready clients and creating the engagement record that captures the rationale.
>
> Fourth, **Session activity tracking** in The Mentoring — recording the sessions occurring within active engagements.
>
> The reasoning: if any of these stops, your mission breaks in a specific way. Without a way to enroll mentors, matching has nothing to match. Without a way to enroll clients, same problem. Without matching, the central operational activity of your mission isn't happening. Without session tracking, the pairing exists only in name — there's no evidence the structured engagement is actually occurring.
>
> Outside the backbone, we'd defer everything else to later iterations: the rest of mentor lifecycle (full onboarding/training, active management, departure), most of client work (outreach, engagement monitoring, formal closure), all of partner relationships, all of donors and funding, and all of workshops and events.
>
> What we're looking for from you in this conversation is: did we get the backbone right? Are there processes we classified as deferred that you think have to be in iteration 1? Are there processes in the backbone you think we could safely defer? Where did we get the handoffs wrong?
>
> Because no other engagement of this type has been done with this methodology, this proposal is grounded in operational analysis rather than accumulated patterns. Places where your instinct conflicts with our proposal are particularly worth pursuing.

### 3.3 Client's initial response

The client's initial response was to confirm the four mission-critical processes match their understanding of the central operational thread. *"You've got the matching at the center, and you've got the two intake paths feeding it, and you've got the session tracking that makes the pairing real. That's right. That's how the work happens."*

The client raised three specific points to discuss:

1. The combinations in Mentor Application & Initial Activation and Client Application & Intake — were they really one process each or two?
2. The Donors/Funding domain being entirely deferred
3. Engagement Closing being supporting rather than mission-critical

These were addressed in turn.

### 3.4 Process combinations (G-007, G-008) — discussion

> **Client:** You combined Application and Initial Activation into one process for mentors. Application and Intake into one process for clients. In how we actually do the work, those feel like two steps each. Application happens, then there's a separate review/screening conversation, then activation. Are we losing something by combining them?
>
> **Consultant:** Good question — this was a judgment call I want to be honest about. The combination is for iteration 1 only. Application and screening and initial activation can absolutely live as separate steps in your operational reality; the question is whether they need to be separate *processes* in the iteration 1 backbone. The answer depends on whether the steps are loosely coupled in time (someone applies, time passes, someone reviews, time passes, someone activates) or tightly coupled (it's all one workflow). Which is closer to how it actually works at CBM?
>
> **Client:** Loosely coupled. Time passes between application and review — sometimes weeks. Time passes between review and activation. They're not one workflow.
>
> **Consultant:** Then I'd take your push and update the backbone. Iteration 1 carries them as separate processes: Mentor Application as one process producing a record marked "applied," Mentor Initial Activation as a second process moving that record to "available for matching." Same split for clients: Client Application as one, Client Intake as a second.
>
> **Client:** That's better.
>
> **Consultant:** It expands the backbone from four processes to six. That's still a tractable iteration 1, and it matches your operational reality more honestly. **G-007 and G-008 in our gap log are now resolved** — we'll record this as the client preferring separate forms, with the new backbone reflecting that.

This was substantive client pushback that materially changed the backbone. The consultant accepted it without resistance because the client's framing (loosely coupled in time) was a real operational distinction the simplification had elided.

**The proposed backbone is now six processes, not four:** Mentor Application, Mentor Initial Activation, Client Application, Client Intake, Matching, Session activity tracking.

### 3.5 Verbatim — Donors/Funding pushback

> **Client:** Now I want to push back on something else. You've got all of Donors/Funding deferred. I understand the priority test says it's not mission-critical for matching mentors and clients — that's true. But practically, we owe our donors reports. Without donor recording happening, the reports we have to produce for our funders are missing data. That's not nothing.
>
> **Consultant:** That's a fair concern. Let me be careful about what "deferred" means here, because I think we may be talking about two different things.
>
> The way the methodology defines deferred: not on the critical path for iteration 1, and not in queue for the next iteration either. Parked. Supporting means: not in iteration 1, but explicitly queued for iteration 2.
>
> What I'm hearing from you is that donor recording isn't on the critical path for iteration 1's mentor-client matching work — but it's also not something you want parked indefinitely. It's something you want sooner rather than later because of your reporting obligations. Is that right?
>
> **Client:** Yes. We need the matching working first, that's right. But donor recording can't wait six months either.
>
> **Consultant:** Then I'd suggest the following. Donor recording moves from deferred to supporting. That means iteration 2 picks it up rather than it being parked. The other donor processes — outreach, stewardship, reporting — can stay deferred for now because they depend on having donor recording in place first. We'll come back to those in iteration 3 or whenever donor recording is bedded in.
>
> Donor recording stays out of iteration 1 because adding it would expand iteration 1's scope past what we can deploy quickly, and the test of iteration 1 is whether your matching system works for mentor-client. If we wait six months to deploy iteration 1 because we're also building donor recording, we lose the iteration speed advantage.
>
> **Client:** I can live with that. Iteration 2 happens within a reasonable time after iteration 1?
>
> **Consultant:** That's how the iterative model is supposed to work. Iteration cadence depends on how fast iteration 1 reveals what needs adjusting, but it's measured in weeks, not months.

The deferred-not-dismissed framing was effective. The client accepted "supporting" as a real commitment rather than a polite way of saying no.

**The classification update:** Donor Recording moves from deferred to supporting. The other three Donors/Funding processes (donor outreach, stewardship, reporting) stay deferred because they depend on donor recording. **No backbone change for iteration 1; the change is to the supporting/deferred classification list.**

### 3.6 Engagement Closing — clarifying conversation

The client's third concern was about Engagement Closing being supporting rather than mission-critical:

> **Client:** Engagement Closing matters for our impact reports. When an engagement closes, we capture the outcome — did the client achieve their goals, what did they accomplish, what was the impact. That's data we need. If it's supporting, will it be there?
>
> **Consultant:** Yes. Supporting means iteration 2's candidate, not deferred. And there's a way to make iteration 1 work without formal engagement closing being a defined process: in iteration 1, an engagement that closes can be marked closed manually, with whatever notes the staff member wants to capture. That's not as good as a defined closure-with-outcome-tracking process, but it's not zero — the data is captured, just less structured.
>
> When we get to iteration 2, defining the closure process formally will be one of the first things we do because of exactly the reason you raised. It serves your reporting obligations.
>
> **Client:** That works.

No backbone change. The classification stands.

### 3.7 What did not get pushed back on

The client did not push back on:

- **All Workshops/Events being deferred.** The client accepted that workshops are real but supplementing per their own Session 1 framing.
- **All Partner Relationships being deferred.** The client noted their own framing in Session 1 that partner work is its own area but did not assert it had to be in iteration 1.
- **Mentor outreach being supporting rather than mission-critical.** The client understood that iteration 1 can deploy with whatever mentor population CBM has at engagement start.
- **The cross-domain dependency framing.** The client appeared to find it natural that mentor enrollment, client intake, and matching are jointly in iteration 1 because the work cannot be done end-to-end without all three. **This is worth noting:** the cross-domain dependency was the methodology's signature surfacing, and the client accepted it without confusion.

After Part B:

| Client Intake | Bringing in Clients | Move "applied" client records to "ready for matching" after qualification |

## 4. Part C — CRM Candidate Review

### 4.1 Summary

The consultant presented the three-CRM candidate set. The client confirmed two of the three immediately and pushed back on CiviCRM on senior-accessibility grounds. After a focused discussion the client agreed to keep CiviCRM in the set with the understanding that the comparison itself would test the senior-accessibility concern. The candidate set was confirmed at three CRMs. Multi-deploy mode was confirmed.

### 4.2 Verbatim — candidate set proposal opening

> **Consultant:** Based on the constraints you mentioned in Session 1 — open-source preferred, cost-sensitive, self-hostable where capacity exists, senior-accessible UI as a hard requirement, browser-based, workflow automation without code, Google Workspace integration, WordPress integration via REST API — we're proposing we deploy iteration 1 to these three candidates: **EspoCRM, CiviCRM, and SuiteCRM.**
>
> Each represents a meaningfully different approach. EspoCRM is a modern lightweight standalone CRM. CiviCRM is a nonprofit-specialized CRM with native fundraising and events that requires a CMS host — and you have WordPress, so the constraint is met. SuiteCRM is a traditional general-purpose CRM with a mature ecosystem.
>
> All three are open-source, all three are self-hostable, all three meet the FOSS-First principle. The reason for multi-deploy: your team will be able to use the iteration 1 functionality on each system, with your real processes and your real terminology. That comparison will be much more useful for selecting a CRM than reading feature lists or watching vendor demos.
>
> We can drop any of these or add others if there are products you specifically want to evaluate. We can also do single-deploy if you've already chosen a CRM. What are your reactions?

### 4.3 Client engagement with the candidate set

> **Client:** EspoCRM and SuiteCRM I'm fine with. CiviCRM I'm not sure about.
>
> **Consultant:** What's the concern with CiviCRM specifically?
>
> **Client:** I've seen CiviCRM. The interface is dated. We've talked about senior-accessible UI being a hard requirement — our mentors include people in their seventies who'll struggle with anything that isn't very straightforward. CiviCRM is going to feel hard to them.
>
> **Consultant:** That's a real concern, and it's something the comparison itself would test directly. Here's the trade-off as I see it: if we drop CiviCRM, we're making that judgment for you based on impression, before iteration 1 actually deploys. If we keep CiviCRM in, you and your mentors will get hands-on experience with it on your real processes, and you'll have a definitive answer about whether the senior-accessibility concern holds up in practice.
>
> The point of multi-deploy is that we don't have to pre-judge. We can let the comparison settle questions like this empirically.
>
> **Client:** And it's not extra work for us to evaluate it?
>
> **Consultant:** It's CRM Builder doing the deployment, not your team. Your team's evaluation effort is roughly the same whether we have two candidates or three — you walk through your work on each and notice what's different. The marginal cost of the third candidate is small.
>
> **Client:** Then keep it in. If the senior-accessibility issue is as bad as I think, the comparison will surface it cleanly.
>
> **Consultant:** Agreed. We'll deploy to all three, and we'll capture what you and your mentors find when you actually use them.

### 4.4 New gap

The client's specific concern about CiviCRM's UI on senior-accessibility grounds is itself a finding worth noting — it's a constraint that should inform what aspects of the iteration 1 deployment to surface during evaluation. **G-009 in the gap log captures this:** the senior-accessibility evaluation criterion needs to be explicit during iteration 1 use, not implicit, because the client has a strong prior that one candidate may fail on it.

### 4.5 Output — confirmed Initial CRM Candidate Set

Three candidates: EspoCRM, CiviCRM, SuiteCRM. Multi-deploy mode confirmed. The candidate set document records the senior-accessibility concern as an evaluation criterion to surface during iteration 1 use.

## 6. Session 2 Was Successful but Not Frictionless

Worth capturing honestly: this was not a Session 2 where the client rubber-stamped the consultant's proposal. Three substantive engagements occurred:

- **Process combinations** — the client pushed back on the G-007/G-008 simplifications, citing operational reality (loose coupling in time). The consultant accepted the push and expanded the backbone from four to six processes. **The client's pushback materially changed the backbone.**
- **Donors/Funding deferral** — the client pushed back on full deferral, citing donor reporting obligations. The consultant deployed the deferred-not-dismissed framing and the donor recording process moved from deferred to supporting (iteration 2 candidate). **The client's pushback changed the supporting/deferred line but did not add anything to iteration 1.**
- **CiviCRM in the candidate set** — the client questioned CiviCRM's fitness on senior-accessibility grounds. The consultant defended multi-deploy as the way to settle that question empirically. **The candidate set survived intact, with senior-accessibility flagged as an evaluation criterion.**

In all three cases, the consultant's response was either to accept the client's correction (process combinations) or to reframe the disagreement to surface a third option (donors moving from deferred to supporting; senior-accessibility becoming an evaluation criterion). No instance of "let me explain why you're wrong."

This is healthy. The methodology's value is in producing a backbone that the client engages with substantively, not one that the client confirms reflexively.

### 7.5 Reflection on what worked and what didn't

**What worked:**

- The confident-proposal opening (§3.2) landed as designed. The client engaged with the proposal substantively rather than reflexively.
- The deferred-not-dismissed framing (§3.5) effectively handled the Donors/Funding pushback without forcing an unwanted scope expansion.
- The cross-domain dependency framing (§3.7 implicit) was accepted by the client without confusion. The methodology's signature surfacing landed cleanly.
- The multi-deploy reframing of the CiviCRM concern (§4.3) converted a candidate-set debate into an evaluation-criterion clarification, which is a more productive form of the same conversation.

**What was harder:**

- The G-007/G-008 simplifications failed at first contact with the client. This is the right outcome — the consultant's between-sessions judgment call was tested and found wanting, and the test surfaced it cleanly. But it's worth noting that those simplifications were exactly the kind of consultant judgment call that the methodology relies on, and one of them (or both) could have survived to iteration 1 in a less engaged client engagement and produced an iteration 1 deployment that didn't match operational reality.
- The simulated client may have been more articulate about the temporal coupling distinction (§3.4) than a real client would be. A real client might have said "those feel like two things" without being able to articulate "loosely coupled in time" so cleanly. The consultant's job in a real engagement may include drawing that distinction out from less precise client language.

**What is unfinished:**

- Phase 1 is complete, but the value of Phase 1 is in what it enables for Phases 2–5. The redo doesn't go that far. Whether the new methodology's iteration loop actually delivers as promised is a question this experiment can't answer — it can only answer whether Phase 1 produced a workable starting point.
