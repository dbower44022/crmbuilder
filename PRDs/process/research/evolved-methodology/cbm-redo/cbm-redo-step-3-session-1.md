## 1. Session Opening

The simulated consultant began by introducing the session structure. Per the Phase 1 guide, this is brief — the consultant frames the conversation as a working session to identify what CBM does operationally, what the major areas of work are, and what the constraints around technology selection look like, with the explicit goal of leaving the session with a draft mission statement, a preliminary domain list, and notes for the consultant's between-sessions work.

The consultant noted that priority classifications would not be discussed in Session 1; that's between-sessions work CBM Builder produces and brings back to Session 2.

The simulated client confirmed readiness to proceed.

## 2. Part A — Operational Mission

### 2.1 Summary

The conversation in Part A moved cleanly from CBM's stated mission language to operational language. The simulated consultant tested the operational characterization with the priority test ("if X stopped tomorrow, would the mission stop"), and the client confirmed the operational center is matching mentors to clients and supporting that pairing through structured engagements.

The consultant probed Question 1 from pre-engagement (operational vs. aspirational). The simulated client confirmed CBM is in active build-out toward Year 1 targets — currently operating but not at full target scale. Some processes are working in practice; others are still being established. This was acknowledged and Part A continued.

The consultant probed Question 2 from pre-engagement (workshops/clinics relative to mentoring). The simulated client confirmed that mentoring is the operational center and workshops are real but supplementing.

### 2.2 Verbatim — Mission translation moment

> **Consultant:** The materials describe CBM as a nonprofit providing free, confidential, impartial mentoring and practical business education to entrepreneurs, small businesses, and nonprofits exclusively in Northeast Ohio. To make sure I understand it operationally — when you think about what your organization concretely does on a Tuesday morning, what's happening?
>
> **Client:** Our team is matching mentors to clients who've come in needing help — that's the core of it. We're also tracking how those engagements are going, fielding new mentor applications, working with our partner organizations, and sometimes putting on workshops or clinics. But the heart of what we do is the matching and the mentoring.
>
> **Consultant:** So the central operational activity is matching mentors to clients and then supporting that pairing over time. Workshops, clinics, and partner work are real but supplementing the mentoring relationship?
>
> **Client:** That's right. The mentoring is the headline. Everything else is in service of making the mentoring work or extending its reach.
>
> **Consultant:** I'd write the operational mission as: *"CBM matches volunteer mentors with entrepreneurs, small businesses, and nonprofits in Northeast Ohio, and supports those mentoring engagements through structured pairings — at no cost to clients."* Does that capture it?
>
> **Client:** Yes. I'd add "confidential" — that's not just a value statement, it's a real operating constraint. People share things with mentors they wouldn't share publicly.
>
> **Consultant:** Agreed. Adjusted: *"CBM matches volunteer mentors with entrepreneurs, small businesses, and nonprofits in Northeast Ohio, and supports those mentoring engagements as confidential, free, structured pairings."*
>
> **Client:** That's right.

### 2.3 Verbatim — Priority test push-back

> **Consultant:** Just to make sure I have this right — if you stopped matching mentors and clients tomorrow, you wouldn't be doing your mission anymore. Yes?
>
> **Client:** That's right. We could stop doing workshops, stop doing partner outreach, stop the events — and we'd still be CBM, just smaller. If we stopped matching mentors and clients, we wouldn't exist as CBM anymore.

### 2.4 Output — drafted Mission Statement

**Operational mission:** *"CBM matches volunteer mentors with entrepreneurs, small businesses, and nonprofits in Northeast Ohio, and supports those mentoring engagements as confidential, free, structured pairings."*

**Aspirational context:** CBM's stated aspiration extends beyond operations: providing "practical business education" through workshops and clinics, building "resilient enterprises that benefit our communities," and operating as part of a Northeast Ohio ecosystem of trusted partners. These are real but secondary to the operational mission.

**Priority test framing:** *A process is mission-critical if its absence would prevent CBM from matching volunteer mentors with clients in Northeast Ohio and supporting those engagements as confidential, free, structured pairings.*

This draft is ready for refinement and confirmation in Session 2.

## 3. Part B — Domain Identification

### 3.1 Summary

The conversation moved from operational mission to identifying the major areas of work CBM does to deliver that mission. The simulated client named six areas in their own language. The consultant tested two of them with the domain-vs-activity test (workshops/events specifically, and partner relationships) and concluded both are domains in their own right based on the client's responses.

The simulated client did not use the structural names from the original CBM methodology (MN, MR, CR, FU), nor the formal terms "Mentor Recruitment" or "Client Recruiting." The client used natural language: "the actual mentoring," "finding and onboarding mentors," "finding and bringing in clients," "partner work," "the donor and funding side," and "workshops and events."

This produced a working list of six domains, one more than the original methodology landed on (which combined workshops/events under Client Recruiting). The consultant captured both lists for between-sessions reconciliation rather than collapsing the client's six into a different number.

### 3.2 Verbatim — Domain identification opening

> **Consultant:** Now thinking about that operational mission — matching mentors and clients, supporting those engagements. What are the major areas of work that requires? What are the big jobs your organization does to make that matching happen?
>
> **Client:** A few different things, all going at the same time. We have to keep our mentor pool healthy — that means recruiting them, getting them through application and onboarding, training, keeping them engaged once they're active, and handling it when they leave. That's a lot of work.
>
> Then there's getting clients into our program — the outreach, the application, the intake conversation where we figure out if we can actually help them, the partner referrals that bring people to us.
>
> Then the actual mentoring once we've got a match — the matching itself, the regular sessions, tracking how it's going, knowing when to step in if it's gone quiet, closing it out when it's done.
>
> Partners are their own thing — we have relationships with maybe a dozen organizations across Northeast Ohio that refer clients to us, sometimes refer mentors, sometimes co-host events. Managing those relationships takes work.
>
> Donors and funding — we're free to clients, so we depend on donors, sponsors, sometimes grants. Stewardship of those relationships, recording contributions, putting together the impact reports they want to see.
>
> And workshops and events — clinics, office hours, sometimes joint events with partners. Less central than the one-to-one mentoring but still a real part of what we do.
>
> **Consultant:** So six areas: keeping the mentor pool healthy, bringing clients into the program, the actual mentoring, partner relationships, donors and funding, and workshops and events. Did I miss anything?
>
> **Client:** No, that's the work.

### 3.3 Verbatim — Domain-vs-activity testing for workshops/events

> **Consultant:** I want to test something. Workshops and events — is that something you do as part of supporting mentoring (workshops to teach mentors, or events that mentors and clients attend together), or is it a separate area of work entirely?
>
> **Client:** It's its own thing. Some workshops are educational — open to clients whether they have a mentor or not. Some are open to the public. Some are co-hosted with partners. They're not part of mentoring; they're a separate way we deliver value.
>
> **Consultant:** Okay. So workshops and events is a domain in its own right. Got it.

### 3.4 Verbatim — Domain-vs-activity testing for partner relationships

> **Consultant:** Same question for partners. Is partner relationship management work your team does as a separate thing, or is it more a flavor of how mentoring happens — partners refer clients in, partners refer mentors in?
>
> **Client:** Partners feel separate enough that we have someone whose role is partner work specifically. They negotiate the partner agreements, track which partners are active, coordinate joint events, handle the referral relationships. That's not just one of the things our mentoring coordinator does — it's its own role. So I'd call it its own area.
>
> **Consultant:** That's a clear answer. Partners is a domain.

### 3.5 What the client did not name

The simulated client did not name as separate domains:

- **Reporting / impact measurement.** This was implicit in the donors-and-funding description ("putting together the impact reports they want to see") but was not named as a separate area. Worth flagging — in some org types impact reporting is a domain unto itself.
- **Internal operations / governance.** The client did not name finance, HR, board governance, or other internal-operations work. This is consistent with what a CRM-focused engagement would expect — internal ops is typically out of CRM scope.
- **Communications.** External communications (newsletters, social media, etc.) was not named. The client may consider this part of marketing-within-Client-Recruiting or it may simply not be on their CRM map.

### 3.6 Output — preliminary Domain Inventory

The simulated consultant captured six domains, in the client's natural language. Generated structural codes are the consultant's working notation for between-sessions tracking only; the client's language is the authoritative form.

| Working code | Client's language | One-line description |
|---|---|---|
| Domain-1 | The Mentoring | Matching mentors and clients, supporting the engagement through to completion |
| Domain-2 | The Mentor Pool | Recruiting, vetting, onboarding, and retaining the volunteer mentor population |
| Domain-3 | Bringing in Clients | Outreach to and intake of new clients into the program |
| Domain-4 | Partner Relationships | Managing the network of partner organizations |
| Domain-5 | Donors and Funding | Donor and sponsor relationships and contribution recording |
| Domain-6 | Workshops and Events | Educational programming and event management |

This list is preliminary — refinement happens between sessions and confirmation in Session 2.

### 3.7 Comparison note (consultant's between-sessions material, not shared with client)

The original CBM methodology landed on four domains: MN (Mentoring), MR (Mentor Recruitment), CR (Client Recruiting — which absorbs partners, marketing, events, and reactivation as sub-domains), and FU (Fundraising).

The simulated client identified six domains. The notable difference: the client experiences *partner relationships*, *workshops and events*, and *bringing in clients* as three separate areas of work, where the original methodology folded all three under CR with sub-domain structure. The client also separated *the mentoring itself* from *the mentor pool* the way the original methodology did (MN vs. MR), so that split survives.

This is a real difference between the methodologies' outputs at this stage. The consultant captures both interpretations for Session 2's discussion — neither is automatically right; they're testing different ways of carving up the same operational reality. The new methodology lets the client's natural categorization stand unless the workability check (between sessions) reveals it doesn't compose cleanly.

## 4. Part C — Process Surfacing

### 4.1 Summary

For each of the six domains identified in Part B, the consultant asked what specific activities people do. The client surfaced processes in operational language. The consultant captured process names without deep-diving (per the Phase 1 guide §3.5 discipline).

This is the part of Session 1 most prone to over-elicitation under the original methodology, and the consultant deliberately resisted following any process beyond identification. Two times the client offered to "walk through" a process in detail; both times the consultant acknowledged interest and redirected.

The processes named below are in the client's language. Mapping to the original methodology's process names is a between-sessions consultant exercise, not a Session 1 deliverable.

### 4.2 Verbatim — process surfacing within "The Mentoring" domain

> **Consultant:** Let's go through "the mentoring" domain. What are the specific things people do in that area?
>
> **Client:** When a client has been brought in and is ready, the matching coordinator looks at the available mentors and figures out who'd be the right fit — based on the client's industry, what they need help with, the mentor's expertise and availability. That's matching.
>
> Once matched, the mentor and client meet — first introduction, then regular sessions over the course of the engagement. We're tracking that those meetings are happening, that the relationship is healthy, that progress is being made.
>
> If the engagement goes quiet — sessions stop happening — we have a process for noticing that and intervening. Sometimes the client has dropped off; sometimes the mentor has; sometimes both. We try to revive it or formally close it.
>
> Eventually engagements close. Could be successful completion, could be early termination because the client moved on or the mentor couldn't continue. Either way we record the outcome and capture what we can about impact.
>
> **Consultant:** Got it. So in "the mentoring": matching, the regular session activity, monitoring engagements that go quiet, and closing them out. Is there a separate "intake" step where the client is brought in, or is that part of bringing in clients?
>
> **Client:** Intake is its own thing. The client is qualified, brought into the program, and made ready for matching — that all happens before the matching coordinator picks them up. So I'd put intake in "bringing in clients," not in "the mentoring." But I can see the case either way; the boundary is fuzzy.
>
> **Consultant:** Okay, fuzzy boundary noted. We'll treat intake as part of bringing in clients for now, and revisit if the workability check between sessions shows it should sit elsewhere.

### 4.3 Process surfacing — "The Mentor Pool" domain

The client described five activities:

- **Outreach to potential mentors** — proactive recruiting, sometimes through partner channels, sometimes through professional associations
- **Application and screening** — the application form, the screening conversation, sometimes a background check
- **Onboarding and training** — completing required training, getting the mentor activated, sometimes a provisional or co-mentoring period before full activation
- **Active mentor management** — tracking which mentors are active, available, at capacity, on hold; tracking dues
- **Departure** — handling when a mentor leaves, voluntarily or otherwise; preserving their record for historical purposes

No deep-dives. Each was captured as a process name with one-sentence summary.

### 4.4 Process surfacing — "Bringing in Clients" domain

The client described three activities, with explicit acknowledgment that the boundaries are less crisp here than for the mentor pool:

- **Outreach to potential clients** — sometimes direct, often through partner referrals, sometimes through workshops that surface interested parties
- **Application** — the client filling out the application form
- **Intake / qualification** — the conversation where CBM determines whether the client is a fit and the program can help, and prepares them for matching

The client noted: *"There's also a step where if a client comes in and isn't a fit right now — maybe their stage of business doesn't match what we offer — we want to keep them in our system so we can re-engage later. That's not really intake; it's more like reactivation when the time is right."*

The consultant captured this as a possible fourth activity (reactivation) with the note that it might be its own process or might be part of the intake activity. **Gap log entry G-001** captures this ambiguity.

### 4.5 Process surfacing — "Partner Relationships" domain

The client described four activities:

- **Identifying and approaching potential partners** — outreach to organizations that might refer clients, refer mentors, or co-host programming
- **Negotiating partnership agreements** — formal agreements about what each party contributes
- **Ongoing partner management** — tracking which partners are active, the volume of activity through each, the health of the relationship
- **Joint events / co-programming** — coordinating shared workshops, events, or referral campaigns

The client noted that joint events overlap with the workshops-and-events domain — *"the same event might be a workshop and a partner-co-hosted thing."* **Gap log entry G-002** captures this overlap.

### 4.6 Process surfacing — "Donors and Funding" domain

The client described four activities:

- **Donor and sponsor outreach** — cultivating relationships with potential funders
- **Recording contributions** — capturing donations, sponsorships, grant disbursements as they come in
- **Stewardship** — acknowledgments, regular touchpoints with active donors, recognition of giving
- **Reporting to funders** — periodic impact reports, grant reports, board-level summaries

The client noted: *"Some of this is the executive director's work, not really the program team's. The board does some of it too. The CRM matters for tracking the data; not all of the relationship work is system-driven."* **Gap log entry G-003** captures the question of whether donor work is fully in CRM scope or partially out.

### 4.7 Process surfacing — "Workshops and Events" domain

The client described three activities:

- **Planning and scheduling** — what events run, when, with what topic, by whom
- **Registration and attendance tracking** — capturing who registered, who showed up, contact information
- **Follow-up** — converting workshop attendees into prospective clients, prospective mentors, or prospective donors

The client noted: *"Follow-up is where the workshop value lands. A workshop where we get fifty attendees but no follow-up is mostly wasted."* The follow-up process appears to bridge into multiple other domains (clients, mentors, donors). **Gap log entry G-004** captures this cross-domain connection.

### 4.8 Discipline note — process detail attempts

Two times during Part C, the client offered to walk through a process in detail (the matching process specifically, and the engagement monitoring process). Both times the consultant declined per Phase 1 guide §3.5:

> **Consultant:** That sounds like an important process — matching is at the center of what you do. I'd like to learn more about it in detail when we get to that iteration. For now I want to make sure I have all the major processes named so we can prioritize together.

This discipline holds. The consultant did not capture process detail; only process names.

### 4.9 Output — process surfacing notes

Twenty-one processes identified across the six domains, captured in the client's language with brief descriptions. Detail deferred to later phases for whichever processes make iteration scope. Mapping to original-methodology process names is between-sessions work for the consultant.

## 5. Part D — Pre-Existing CRM Context and Constraints

### 5.1 Summary

Part D was short — about twelve minutes of conversation. The simulated client confirmed CBM has stated technology principles, has done some informal evaluation of CRM options, but has not committed to a specific product. The client confirmed the constraints anticipated from pre-engagement reading and added a few specifics.

The consultant probed Question 4 (confidentiality as constraint), Question 5 (funding model and constraints), and Question 6 (technology team capacity and mentor demographics) from the pre-engagement question list. All three produced useful operational detail.

### 5.2 Verbatim — confidentiality constraint specifics

> **Consultant:** Confidentiality recurs across all your materials. I want to make sure I understand operationally: when you say confidential, who can see what, and what would be a violation?
>
> **Client:** Mentoring conversations and what clients share with mentors are private to that pairing. Other mentors shouldn't see what's discussed between mentor X and client Y. Administrators need to see enough to manage engagements but not the conversation contents themselves. Donors and partners should never see specific client information without explicit permission.
>
> A violation would be: someone who shouldn't see a client's situation seeing it. Or partner information ending up on a donor's report. Or a mentor's profile being visible to clients beyond their own assigned mentor in ways the mentor didn't agree to.
>
> The system has to support visibility rules that match these expectations. We can't just have everyone-sees-everything.

### 5.3 Verbatim — technology team capacity

> **Consultant:** The Master PRD mentions one tech-capable administrator as the Year 1 staff. Is that one person who handles CRM administration alongside other duties, or someone whose specific role is technology?
>
> **Client:** It's one person who has technology capability among other administrative responsibilities. They're capable but stretched. Whatever we put in place can't require constant developer-level maintenance.
>
> **Consultant:** And the mentors and clients who interact with the system — what's their technology comfort level?
>
> **Client:** Variable. We have mentors in their seventies who use email and a web browser comfortably and would struggle with a mobile app. We have clients across a wide range — some are tech-fluent, some are not. The lowest common denominator matters because we don't get to choose who gets in. Senior-accessible UI is a real constraint, not a nice-to-have.

### 5.4 Verbatim — funding model and operational scope

> **Consultant:** You operate as free-to-client, which means external funding. Is donor and funder relationship management work your team does today, or is that primarily handled by the board or executive team?
>
> **Client:** Mix of both. The board owns the strategic donor relationships — major donors, foundations, the cultivation of new significant funders. The executive director and program team handle the operational side — recording contributions, sending acknowledgments, day-to-day stewardship of mid-tier donors, putting together the reports that the board uses.
>
> The CRM needs to support the operational team's work. The board's strategic relationship work doesn't need to be in the CRM, except where it touches data the operational team owns.

### 5.5 Output — CRM context notes

**CBM-stated technology principles** (from pre-engagement reading, confirmed by client in Session 1):

- FOSS-First — open-source preferred over commercial; commercial only when no FOSS alternative exists
- Cost Tiering — start with free or near-free; clear upgrade triggers as the org grows
- Data privacy and confidentiality — non-negotiable, with specific visibility rules required
- Avoid vendor lock-in — free data export required
- Simplicity over features — "can a non-technical senior volunteer use this confidently with minimal training?"
- Local control — self-hosting preferred where capacity exists
- Volunteer-friendly and senior-accessible UI — browser-based preferred; minimal apps; clean readable interfaces

**Operational team capacity:** One technology-capable administrator, with administrative responsibilities beyond CRM. No developer-level expertise on staff. Tooling that requires constant developer maintenance is unworkable.

**User base:** Mentors range up to 70s+ in age; technology comfort is variable. Senior-accessible UI is a hard requirement.

**Confidentiality requirement:** Visibility rules required. Not everyone-sees-everything; specific role-based access matching the operational structure.

**Pre-existing CRM evaluations:** CBM has done some informal evaluation of options but has not committed. The client did not name specific products in this conversation. **Gap log entry G-005** captures the consultant's note that prior CRM evaluation documents (in the Archive per Step 1 survey) may have factual constraint content that should inform the CRM Candidate Set drafting.

**Funding scope for CRM:** The operational donor work (recording contributions, stewardship of mid-tier donors, reports for the board) is in CRM scope. The strategic donor relationship work (major donor cultivation, new significant funder development) is largely board-and-executive-director work and is not centrally CRM-driven, except where it consumes data the operational team owns. **Gap log entry G-006** captures this scope boundary.

## 6. End-of-Session Synthesis

The simulated consultant summarized back to the client:

- **Drafted operational mission:** *"CBM matches volunteer mentors with entrepreneurs, small businesses, and nonprofits in Northeast Ohio, and supports those mentoring engagements as confidential, free, structured pairings."*
- **Six domains identified** in the client's language (the working table from §3.6)
- **Twenty-one processes** named across the six domains
- **CRM context** including the seven stated principles, the team-capacity constraint, the user-base accessibility requirement, the confidentiality visibility requirement, and the funding-scope boundary

The consultant asked: *"Does anything in here look wrong, or did we miss something major?"*

The client confirmed the summary was accurate, with one note: *"I want to make sure when we come back next time you're still treating the mentoring itself and the mentor pool as separate domains. Some people who come in want to collapse them and I don't think that works."*

The consultant acknowledged the note and confirmed the separation would survive in the proposed backbone unless the workability check surfaced a reason to revisit it.

The consultant told the client what was coming next:

> **Consultant:** I'm going to take all of this and prepare a proposed prioritized backbone — what we'd build first, what we'd defer, and how we'd sequence things. I'll also propose two or three CRM products to deploy your iteration 1 to in parallel so you can compare them with your real processes running. We'll meet again in about a week to walk through both proposals together.

The client confirmed the timeline. Session 1 closed.

## 7. Step 3 Outputs

### 7.1 What Step 3 produced

- **Drafted Mission Statement** (§2.4) — operational mission language ready for Session 2 confirmation
- **Preliminary Domain Inventory** (§3.6) — six domains in the client's language with working codes for between-sessions consultant tracking
- **Process surfacing notes** (§4) — twenty-one processes across six domains, captured at name + one-sentence-description level
- **CRM context notes** (§5.5) — seven stated principles, capacity constraints, user-base requirements, confidentiality requirements, scope boundary
- **Six gap log entries** (G-001 through G-006) — recorded in `cbm-redo-gap-log.md`

### 7.2 What Step 4 (between-sessions) needs from Step 3

Step 4 produces the proposed Prioritized Backbone and Initial CRM Candidate Set per Phase 1 guide §4. Step 4 needs from Step 3:

- The six-domain working list, with the consultant's between-sessions task of classifying each contained process as mission-critical, supporting, or deferred
- The workability check applied to the mission-critical set: *can a real CBM staff member do their actual job for one realistic case from start to finish using only the mission-critical processes?*
- The CRM context notes informing the candidate set selection
- The gap log informing which proposals get marked low-confidence

The notable open question for Step 4: the simulated client identified six domains where the original methodology landed on four. The consultant in Step 4 should not collapse the client's six to match the original four; the workability check should be done against the client's six, and any domain-structural decisions should be deferred to Session 2 verification.

### 7.3 What worked well in this simulated session

- The Phase 1 guide §3.5 discipline (resist deep-diving into individual processes) held. Both attempts to deep-dive were redirected per the worked example in the guide.
- The mission translation moment (§2.2) produced a one-sentence operational mission cleanly — the client had ready language because the materials supported it.
- The domain-vs-activity testing (§3.3, §3.4) produced clear answers from the client without confusion.
- The end-of-session synthesis was efficient because the four parts had each produced their own captured outputs along the way.

### 7.4 What was harder in this simulated session

- The boundary between "intake" (which the client placed in Bringing in Clients) and "matching" (which the client placed in The Mentoring) was fuzzy and acknowledged as such (§4.2). This is a real ambiguity that the workability check between sessions has to resolve.
- The cross-domain connections (workshops connecting to clients/mentors/donors per §4.7; partner work overlapping with workshops per §4.5; donor work spanning operational team and board per §5.4) are real and will affect the workability check.
- The simulated client had richer ready answers than a real first-engagement client likely would. This reflects the ground rules §5.5 limit: the simulated client's articulateness is artificially high. A real session would have more hesitation, more "let me get back to you," more hedge.
